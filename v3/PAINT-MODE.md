# PAINT-MODE.md — Zen Pong Paint Surface Standing Spec

Cross-brief spec for the paint/splatter surface. These decisions apply across
briefs 11–14 and are referenced by each, not repeated in each. When a brief
conflicts with this file, stop and flag it.

Status of each decision is marked: **LOCKED** (settled, don't revisit),
**PROVISIONAL** (agreed direction, numbers to be tuned by eye in the lab),
**OPEN** (not yet decided, do not build against).

---

## 1. What paint mode is

A third surface alongside paper and chalkboard. The ball behaves as a loaded,
dripping brush: it lays a continuous trail whose width varies along its length,
pools into blobs where it changes direction, and throws discrete splatter marks
driven by its own physics state. At game end the artwork re-renders with a
ground composited beneath the marks — the reveal.

Primary aesthetic target is the **cream-ground / line-dominant** family: a
visible warm substrate, one or two confident looping lines carrying the
composition, a handful of flat accent blobs, fine multi-colour speckle.

**OPEN — density ceiling.** The maximalist references (60–80% coverage,
6–8 simultaneous colours) sit five to eight times above the locked paper
calibration of ~30–35 strokes / 10–12% ink. Either paint gets its own,
much higher calibration number, or those references are aspirational and
the restrained family is the real target. Not resolved. Build to the
restrained family until it is; do not design the renderer in a way that
forecloses the dense one.

## 2. Colour system — selection over a curated library, not arithmetic

**LOCKED.** Palettes are never generated by rotating hues on a colour wheel.
Algorithmic generation produces colours nobody approved, and some fraction of
them will be ugly. Instead: a hand-curated library of hexes is the only source
of colour in the product, and colour-scheme rules act as *selection constraints
over that library* — the rule decides which combinations are permitted, never
what a colour is.

This keeps every hex that can appear in a finished artwork one Shivang chose,
while keeping combination systematic.

### 2.1 Hue library — PROVISIONAL (starting points, eyeball-tune)

Twelve hues at roughly 30° spacing. Index is the hue slot; scheme rules operate
on index arithmetic mod 12.

| # | Name | Hex | ~Hue |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Crimson | `#D92D3C` | 350° |
| 1 | Vermilion | `#E8452A` | 10° |
| 2 | Orange | `#F07818` | 28° |
| 3 | Amber | `#F5A623` | 40° |
| 4 | Yellow | `#F5C518` | 48° |
| 5 | Chartreuse | `#A8C63C` | 75° |
| 6 | Green | `#3FA65A` | 140° |
| 7 | Teal | `#1F8A8C` | 182° |
| 8 | Cyan-blue | `#2E9BD4` | 200° |
| 9 | Blue | `#2B5FD9` | 225° |
| 10 | Violet | `#5B3FC4` | 255° |
| 11 | Magenta | `#C43C8E` | 320° |

Yellow reuses chalk's `#F5C518` deliberately — it read well there.

This library also closes the "12 palette hex values" item open in `BACKLOG.md`.

### 2.2 Ink (line) colour — LOCKED

Near-black, constant across all schemes: `#16120E` — a warm black that sits
with cream rather than fighting it. Every reference in the restrained family
uses a near-black line; this is not a variable. A white-on-dark inversion is
possible later and is out of scope until the ground modes are settled.

### 2.3 Slot budget — LOCKED

Maximum **five colour slots per artwork**:

```
1  ground
2  ink (line)
3  accent A   — dominant
4  accent B   — secondary
5  accent C   — minor
```

Three accents, never more. The restrained references resolve to exactly this
(cream / black / yellow / orange-red / teal). If the dense family is later
approved it gets a documented exception to four accents, not an open ceiling.

### 2.4 Accent weighting — PROVISIONAL

Accents are not used in equal measure. Area weights:

```
A dominant   0.55
B secondary  0.30
C minor      0.15
```

This is what stops the output reading as confetti. In the primary reference the
yellow plainly dominates, the orange-red answers it, the teal appears three or
four times total. Weights govern splatter colour probability, blob colour
assignment, and patch area share.

### 2.5 Scheme rules — PROVISIONAL

A scheme picks three library indices given a seeded base index `i`:

| Scheme | Selection | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Analogous | `i, i+2, i+4` | Calm, close-harmony, low tension |
| Split-complementary | `i, i+5, i+7` | The workhorse — one hue against two near-opposites |
| Triadic | `i, i+4, i+8` | Even, poster-like, high energy |
| Complementary + minor | `i, i+6, i+2` | Strong opposition with one bridging hue |

Analogous was widened from `i, i+1, i+3` to `i, i+2, i+4` in brief 11. The
original selected two adjacent library hues (30° apart) often enough to fail
its own accent-separation floor on 9 of 12 base indices — mis-specified
offsets, not a floor set too high. `i, i+2, i+4` drops the adjacent pair,
keeps all three hues inside a 120° window so the close-harmony character
survives, and clears the same floor as every other scheme.

All arithmetic mod 12. Scheme and base index are both seeded, so a given seed
always yields the same palette.

### 2.6 Separation guards — LOCKED (method), PROVISIONAL (values)

Both use OKLab ΔE. WCAG relative luminance is the wrong tool here — it is a
text-legibility metric, and it cannot see chroma separation, which is the axis
that makes yellow-on-cream work.

| Guard | Value | Role |
|---|---|---|
| `MIN_GROUND_DE` | 0.16 | Dormant by design |
| `MIN_ACCENT_DE` | 0.15 | Active — the guard that does real work |

The ground guard rejects nothing in the current library and is not expected
to: the 12 × 4 matrix showed Yellow/Cream at ΔE 0.1687 is simultaneously the
global minimum *and* the best pairing in the reference set. It exists only as
a tripwire if a pale, low-chroma hue is added to `HUE_LIBRARY` later. Do not
tune it upward to make it appear useful, and never special-case an approved
combination to bypass it — carving out a good result to satisfy a guard proves
the guard is measuring the wrong relationship.

Assignment of A/B/C to the three picked hues is also seeded, not positional —
the base hue should not always be the dominant one.

## 3. Ground modes — LOCKED (two modes), PROVISIONAL (values)

### Mode 1 — Plain

A single flat colour plus canvas-weave texture. **Colour is chosen**, not
random — this is a user-facing product control, in the same family as the
existing colour picker, and is therefore exempt from the lab-controls rule in
`v3/CLAUDE.md`.

Ground set — PROVISIONAL:

| Name | Hex |
|---|---|
| Cream | `#F4EBD4` |
| Warm white | `#FAF6EE` |
| Pale lavender | `#E6E8F2` |
| Blush | `#F2DCDC` |

Accent selection must satisfy a minimum contrast against the chosen ground; a
scheme that fails is re-rolled rather than rendered muddy.

### Mode 2 — Patches

Multiple large soft-edged colour fields. **No colour choice** — fully seeded
and random, by design. Patches draw from the same scheme as the splatters and
blobs, so ground and marks agree rather than clashing.

PROVISIONAL: 3–6 fields, 55–85% total coverage, soft irregular edges, area
share following the A/B/C weights.

### 3.1 Patches are seeded and composition-aware — LOCKED

The patch layer is driven by the same seed as the game and reads the committed
stroke set when placing fields. It is not an independent coin flip. Two
identical rallies must not produce wildly different paintings, and the ground
should land in relation to where the ball actually went — otherwise the ground
is decoration the player had no part in, and "the ball is responsible for the
outcome" stops being true.

---

## 4. The reveal — LOCKED as product direction, OPEN on execution

The ground does not exist during play. The player paints on a neutral
substrate; at game end the artwork re-renders with the ground composited
beneath the marks.

This is free architecturally — v3 renders from stroke data, so compositing
beneath costs nothing.

**OPEN:** the transition itself (hard swap vs. cross-fade, duration, whether
patches wipe in). Deferred to the product port. The lab builds and judges the
static resolved image first; motion is not a lab concern.

Contingency, per Shivang: if the reveal doesn't land in practice, plain mode
can fall back to ground-established-at-start with no engine change — only the
moment of compositing moves.

## 5. Mark taxonomy — PROVISIONAL

Six elements, in build order:

**A. Continuous trail — always on.** The unbroken line is Zen Pong's identity
and the reason the restrained references work compositionally. Emission is
never switched off; variety comes from what the line *does*, not from whether
it exists.

**B. Variable width along the stroke.** Low-frequency modulation across arc
length, simulating a brush loading and unloading.

**C. Pooling at direction change.** Real paint pools where the brush
decelerates. Paddle hits and wall bounces are exactly those moments and already
fire events. Width spikes over a short arc window either side of the commit
point. This produces the knots visible at the turns of every reference loop.

**D. Physics-driven emission events.** Splatter is not a coin flip — it is
driven by the ball state already being carried:

```
high spin   → splatter burst, thrown perpendicular to travel
high speed  → thin whip line, low width, long
slow/heavy  → thick pooled knot, short
```

This makes the artwork a legible record of the rally rather than noise, and is
the same idea as the spin-shape-drops item already logged and endorsed in
`BACKLOG.md`, arriving from a different direction.

**E. Drips.** A short downward tapering stroke hanging off a pool or blob.
Cheap once pooling exists — it is a small stroke from a known anchor point, not
a new system. Promoted out of "stretch" on that basis.

**F. Flat region fills.** The clean line-and-fill reference is close to what
`fill.js` already produces; treat as a later sub-mode reusing existing
enclosed-region detection, not new work.

## 6. Brief arc

| Brief | Scope |
|---|---|
| 11 | Palette module, canvas ground (plain only), variable-width paint stroke with pooling |
| 12 | Physics-driven emission events + drips |
| 13 | Patch ground mode, seeded and composition-aware |
| 14 | Reveal composite; fills sub-mode; density question resolved |

Order is deliberate. Patches are built late despite being the easiest, because
a lush ground would paper over a weak stroke renderer and we would not find out
until much later.
